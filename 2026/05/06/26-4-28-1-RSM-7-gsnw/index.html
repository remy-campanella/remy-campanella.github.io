【RSM】 Chapter 7 Goodbye Snowie

发表于2026-05-06|更新于2026-05-06|艺术文学拙作Reminiscences de Snowflake Mapleleaf
|浏览量:

A hoof shot out, connecting squarely with Snowflake’s shoulder. The blunt impact crumpled her stance, sending her sprawling backward onto the grass. The back of her head hit the ground with a dull thud, and for a moment the sky above her swam in and out of focus.

Snowie dropped her full weight onto her, pinning her flat against the damp earth. Her two front hooves dug into the hollow of Snowflake’s throat—not pressing hard enough to choke, but hard enough to make the point. The weight of her was almost nothing. She was so small. When had she been this small?

“You…”

A fine, violent tremor ran through Snowie’s entire body. Her breath came in short, ragged gasps, the heat of them washing over Snowflake’s cheek. She smelled like grass and something else—something raw and electric, like the air before a storm.

“Why!”

Snowflake didn’t dare to look into Snowie’s eyes. She stared past her, at the grey sky, at nothing.

“I…”

“I adore you as my hero!” Snowie scolded, her voice pitching upward, cracking at the seams, “Why did you lie to me!”

“I… I’m not…”

“I thought the worst thing is to be a loser.” Snowie’s hooves grabbed the chest fluff at Snowflake’s collar and pulled, hard. The pain was horribly realistic—a sharp, burning drag across her skin. “But you are a liar! How dare you! I’m literally you!”

“You’re NOT my future!” Snowie yelled. Her tears started shining in her eyes, catching the dim light like tiny broken mirrors. “I’ll never be like you!”

Snowie raised her hoof above Snowflake’s face. Snowflake didn’t avoid. She lay there, staring up at the small hoof silhouetted against the grey sky, and felt nothing. Not fear, not resistance. Just a vast, hollow agreement.

Yes. You shouldn’t be like me.

Snowie paused. She looked at her trembling hoof, like it didn’t belong to her anymore. For a long, terrible second, she just held it there, shaking. Then she slammed her hoof into the dirt an inch from Snowflake’s left ear. The impact sent dry soil flying, scattering across Snowflake’s cheek and into her mane. Snowie froze, her chest heaving. Then she lifted her weight off Snowflake, pushed herself up, and spun around. Without a backward glance, she melted into the darkness.

“I’m so sorry…” Snowflake mumbled. No one heard her.

Snowflake had no one beside her.

Snowflake had nothing left.

She lay on the grass for a long time, staring at the sky, feeling the damp earth soak through her fur. The only thing she could see when she finally sat up was the reflection of the moon on the river surface—not bright, just a pale smear of light that the water couldn’t hold. The only thing she could hear was the flowing sound of the river water, low and constant, like a voice that had given up on being understood. The only thing she could smell was the coldness of the river bank, a mineral emptiness that got into her nostrils and stayed there.

Snowie was supposed to be the last one to support her. The last piece of herself that hadn’t been ground down or traded away or locked in a box. Now she had lost that chance. Snowie was literally herself. If even Snowflake herself hated Snowflake so much—hated her enough to hit her, to cry, to scream that she would never be like her—then what was the point of her insistence to live on?

Snowflake stood up. She nearly lost her balance. The world tilted, and she had to wait for it to settle before she could trust her legs. She walked beside the river, each step slow and deliberate, like walking through a dream where the ground keeps shifting. The water reflected her face.

Snowflake saw nothing but a demon.

“I deserve it,” Snowflake thought. “I’m not afraid.”

She moved forward into the water.

“And the next day ended like nothing happened.”

That was what Snowie had written in her diary. That was what Snowflake had told herself. The truth was that nothing ended. Everything just continued, rotting quietly in the dark, the way rot does when you seal it inside a wall and pretend the smell isn’t there.

After that night, Snowflake didn’t dare to make her father that mad again. In fact, she began to calculate—obsessively, compulsively—any possibility of making her parents mad, and trying to kill that possibility in advance. She built a fortress outside her mood, thick and high, showing only the feelings she had inspected and approved. Sadness was fine, as long as it was quiet. Frustration was fine, as long as it was directed at a textbook. Anything louder, messier, more real—she sealed it behind the wall and walked away.

The next priority was her study. She decided to improve her score at any cost, so that no one could blame her, no one could look at her and see a disappointment. She could still feel tired—her body would remind her, aching in the early hours—but the moment she stopped, the moment she even thought about stopping, the image of the door would surface. The door closing. The coldness of the ground on the other side. The loneliness of being abandoned in a house that was still full of kirins.

She safely finished junior high, then started senior high. She gave up making friends, thinking they might distract her from studying. Besides, no one wants to know this crazy student anyway—the one who sits alone, who never goes to the cafeteria, who flinches when someone speaks too loudly. Teachers were amazed by her resilience. Westwind and Crimson were also happy to see their daughter had finally grown up. She heard her mother tell a relative once, over the phone, “She’s so mature now. We don’t even need to worry about her.”

But her score had never been improved.

The water submerged her legs. It pressed against her fur, heavy and slow, like bandages being wrapped too tightly. She kept walking.

The only light in Snowflake’s cursed life was music.

Friendship needs effort to maintain—conversations, gestures, the constant, exhausting work of being present for someone else. Music doesn’t. Music just sits there, waiting, asking nothing. Classical music was the only hobby Snowflake had reserved from the old days, the days before the fortress. Her only way to temporarily skip from the reality was letting her heart beat after the tempo. Classical music described a paradise—orderly, beautiful, beyond the reach of her father or tests or cold floors—and she was the believer of that.

To truly embrace its beauty, she sneaked into the school hall every Saturday evening. There stood a concert grand, and she cherished every second with it. She would play for hours, lost in the architecture of sonatas and suites, and for those few hours the thing inside her chest that was constantly screaming would go quiet.

“Bravo.”

Snowflake startled so hard she nearly fell off the bench. She quickly closed the piano lid and hurried off the chair, her heart hammering against her ribs. Down there sat a tall kirin clapping her hooves slowly. Snowflake could see her green mane and grey body in the half-light from the windows.

“I… I’m so sorry! I shouldn’t break into here without permission!”

The kirin walked onto the stage, her steps calm and unhurried. “It’s okay, dear. I’m not here to blame you. Call me Rainshine.”

Snowflake couldn’t believe her ears. “Ms. Rainshine… the music professor visiting our school recently?”

“Yes.” Rainshine nodded. “May I have your name?”

Snowflake lowered her sight. “Snowflake Mapleleaf… I’m so sorry, I shouldn’t bother your visit.”

“Your performance is never a bother, Miss Mapleleaf.” Rainshine smiled. It was a warm smile, the kind Snowflake hadn’t seen directed at her in years. “I have to say you have an in-depth understanding of classical music, but I didn’t see you in those art students.”

“Because I… actually not an art student. I take the normal college entrance examinations.”

“Oh. So how was your normal lessons then?”

Snowflake bit her lips. The music teacher who had silently followed Rainshine into the hall leaned in and whispered something into the professor’s ear. Rainshine’s expression shifted—just slightly, just enough. A micro-adjustment, the kind of look a doctor gives when they glance at a chart and realize the numbers are worse than the patient let on.

“I see.” Rainshine’s voice remained warm, but something underneath it had changed. “Have you ever thought about being an art student?”

Snowflake suddenly looked up as she heard those words. The air in the hall felt different.

“The normal examination isn’t the only way. You deserve better.”

“What?” Snowflake said after a long pause.

“You could shift your aim, and I could arrange you a position in the art exam. It’s all up to you.”

She accepted. She participated. The exam ended smoothly. It was not until the score arrived—folded in her pocket for two full days, worn soft at the edges from being touched and refolded—that she placed it on her father’s desk.

Westwind looked at the paper. Then he looked at her. His brow furrowed, not in anger, but in the slow, careful confusion of someone who realizes a significant piece of information has been kept from him.

“You took the art exam.”

It wasn’t a question.

“I didn’t want to bother you until I knew the result,” Snowflake said. Her voice was flat, rehearsed. She had practiced this sentence in front of the mirror until all the tremor was gone.

Westwind picked up the paper and studied it. Snowflake watched his eyes move across the numbers. The score was good. Not outstanding—not the kind that would make a university fight over her—but solid. Respectable. Enough.

He set the paper down. He was quiet for a long time. Not the dangerous quiet of the old days—the quiet that preceded slamming doors and cold floors. This was a different quiet. Slower. More deliberate. Like he was choosing his words the way someone chooses a path through a minefield.
“When did you decide this?” he asked.

“Earlier this semester.”

“And you didn’t think to discuss it with me first.”

It still wasn’t a question. Snowflake’s shoulders tensed. She knew this tone. She had spent years learning to read the weather of his voice. But something was off. The barometer wasn’t pointing where she expected it to.

“I…” Snowflake started.

Westwind exhaled. He leaned back in his chair and looked at his daughter—really looked, in a way he hadn’t in a long time. He saw the dark circles under her eyes. He saw the way she was standing: weight on her back hooves, body angled slightly toward the door, ready to flinch. Or run.

He saw it. And something in his face shifted. Not softening—nothing so simple. More like a door closing quietly, locking away whatever he had been about to say.

“Your normal scores weren’t improving,” he said. It was a statement of fact, not an accusation. “And you clearly have… something. In this.”
Snowflake said nothing. Her body didn’t unclench.

“If that’s your path,” Westwind said, “then go for it.”

He picked up his newspaper. But he didn’t unfold it right away. He held it in his hooves for a moment, then added, without looking up:

“I’m not angry. I just wish you had told me earlier. So I could have… helped.”

Snowflake nodded. She turned and walked to the door. Her hoof touched the frame.

“Thank you,” she said.

She meant it. She knew he meant it too. And that was the worst part—knowing that he was trying, knowing that the door wasn’t locked anymore, and realizing that she still couldn’t walk through it. The fortress she had built didn’t have a door on her side. It only had walls.

She registered as a student in the Composition Department of East Westplain Associated University[^1].

The water reached her neck. The cold was no longer just pressing—it was squeezing, wrapping around her throat like a slow, patient hand. Each breath took more effort than the last. She kept walking.

Snowflake could deal with those subjects without effort. The theory, the harmony, the counterpoint—they came to her like remembering something she had always known, like finding a room in her own house that she had forgotten existed. But she never considered herself as talented. Talent was for creatures who stood on stages and made audiences weep. Talent was a word for creatures who believed they deserved to be there. She was just good at passing tests. So she worked harder, day and night, to prove to herself—and to the ghosts of her parents’ expectations—that she could still make them proud, even if she wouldn’t be an engineer.

One night, she was working in the piano room. She had been there for hours, her back aching, her eyes burning, the score in front of her blurring at the edges. Then the power died. The fluorescent lights flickered once, twice, and went out, plunging the room into total darkness. Snowflake sat frozen, her hooves still on the keys, holding a sheet of music she could no longer read. Outside the window, some students passed by. Their chat and laughter drifted through the glass, bright and effortless, like they belonged to a different species entirely.

Snowflake sat in the dark for a long time. She didn’t move. She didn’t cry. She just sat there, holding her music score like a drowning kirin holding a piece of driftwood, and listened to the sound of other creatures being alive.

Autumn Blaze was an exception. Snowflake met this literature student on orientation day. Autumn Blaze was also a newbie, but she had somehow managed to weasel her way into the welcome team—bright, loud, chaotic, utterly unafraid of strangers. She had a habit of appearing beside Snowflake’s library table without warning and declaring, with absolute confidence, that Snowflake needed to “experience life” and that she, Autumn Blaze, was going to be the one to make that happen. Snowflake had a nice university time with her only friend. She somehow reached a fragile, unfamiliar thing called a life balance because of Autumn Blaze. It was the closest she had ever come to being happy.

The university time passed fast. The music students around her were ambitious. They planned their lives as future star musicians—concert tours, recordings, fame. Snowflake listened to them and felt nothing. She thought her chance to be in the university was nothing but a coincidence. A fluke. A mistaken door that had accidentally opened for someone who didn’t belong on the other side. She wanted a stable job. Like an engineer. Something with a clear path, a predictable salary, a future that didn’t require her to believe in herself. But Westplain didn’t have many opportunities for that. Finally, she found a job as a primary school music teacher, far away from home.

The water reached her lips. The surface of the stream was perfectly still, inches from her nose. It didn’t ripple. It didn’t invite. It simply existed, like a wall waiting to receive her.

Snowflake realized something, standing there with the waterline at her mouth. No one forced her to study music. No one forced her to be a music teacher. No one forced her to lie to herself, to Snowie, to everyone she had ever known. It was Snowflake who chose to live like that. Every step, every compromise, every “Nothing happened”—she had signed every one. The door had never locked from the outside.

Snowflake opened her mouth. Water flowed through her throat, smooth and cold, filling the spaces where air used to be. It locked her vocal cords. The process was fast. There was no pain at all.

She took another step deeper.

Then something grabbed her mane and pulled.

The force was desperate and clumsy, much too weak to move a full-grown kirin. Snowflake didn’t resist. Her body slid through the water, dragged by something that was half-frantic, half-sobbing. She broke the surface. Air hit her face, sharp and unwelcome. She was hauled onto the bank and left there, dripping, on the grey stones.

Snowie was kneeling beside her, heaving. Her small hooves were shaking, her chest rising and falling in violent spasms. Water dripped from her mane. She had run back—run back into the water, up to her own chest, to grab Snowflake and pull.

Snowie stared at Snowflake’s face. Snowflake’s eyes were open but empty, focused on nothing, the silence curse still clamping down on her throat. She couldn’t speak. She couldn’t even nod.

“Say something!” Snowie’s voice cracked. “Please! Just—just say something! Yell at me! Hit me back! I don’t care, just—”

Snowflake didn’t respond. She lay there like something that had been fished out of the water and hadn’t yet decided whether to keep living.

Snowie’s composure shattered. She grabbed Snowflake’s shoulder with both hooves and shook her—gently at first, then harder, then stopped, because Snowflake’s body just lolled with the motion like a ragdoll.

“I’m sorry.” Snowie’s voice was barely a whisper now. “I’m sorry. I shouldn’t have—I didn’t mean to—”

She pressed her forehead against Snowflake’s wet shoulder. Her whole body was shaking.

“I said you weren’t my future. I said I’d never be like you.” The words came out wet and broken. “I didn’t mean it. I was just— I was so mad, and I was scared, and I said the worst thing I could think of, and I—”

She pulled back and looked at Snowflake’s face again. The anger was gone. What replaced it was something raw and exposed, like a wound without a scab.

“You were trying to protect me, weren’t you?” Snowie said. “That’s why you lied. You thought if I knew how bad it was, I’d—I don’t know—break. You thought you had to be the strong one so I could stay… clean.”

Snowie wiped her nose with the back of her hoof. It was a child’s gesture, graceless and unselfconscious.

“But I’m not clean. I’m not some pure little dream you have to keep in a box. I’m you. I’m supposed to be the one you don’t have to lie to.”

She stopped. Her breath hitched. Then, quieter:

“Please don’t die.”

Snowflake still couldn’t speak. But she didn’t need to. Something in her eyes shifted—a tiny, almost imperceptible change, like a key turning in a lock that had been rusted shut for years.

Snowie sat back on her heels and wiped her face. She was still trembling, but the storm had passed. She looked at Snowflake—really looked—and her expression changed again. This time it wasn’t anger, and it wasn’t grief. It was something slower. Something more careful.

She turned Snowflake’s body around. Her hooves touched Snowflake’s side, and she froze.

Snowflake’s weight was extraordinarily light. Not thin. Not slim. Light. The kind of light that meant there was nothing underneath the skin but bone and exhaustion, a skeleton wrapped in wet fur. Snowie had expected to grab something solid—an adult body, a lived-in body. What she got was a shell.

Snowie picked up Snowflake’s hoof. Her small hooves turned it over slowly, examining it. What she saw made her stomach drop.

The calluses were big—much bigger than her own, much more than a pianist should have. They were thick and layered, the skin hardened into ridges and valleys, concentrated on the inner side of the hoof in a pattern that didn’t match piano keys at all. These were the calluses of someone who had gripped a piece of chalk for years, pressing it into a blackboard day after day with a grip far too tight, always too tight, because she was always, always afraid it would slip. Because everything she did, she did with the desperate, grinding force of someone who believed that if she loosened her grip for even a second, the whole world would fall away.

Then Snowie looked at Snowflake’s face. The dark circles around her eyes were not the kind that came from a single sleepless night. They were deep, sunken, permanent—bruises that had stopped healing and simply become part of the geography of her face. And her eyes themselves—Snowie knew those eyes. She had seen them in the mirror, a long time ago, on the nights after the door closed. That hollow, practiced blankness. Not the absence of feeling, but the presence of a wall so high and so thick that it looked like nothing at all.

Snowie stared for a long time.

She realised it then. Snowflake didn’t make her dream come true. She had tried, and she had failed, and then she had kept going anyway—alone, far from home, far from anyone who knew what she used to be—dragging herself through a grey, grinding existence that she had chosen not because she wanted it, but because she was terrified of wanting anything else. Cheating her was not because Snowflake was cruel. It was because she really, truly believed that if Snowie never saw the ugliness, Snowie could stay clean. Stay whole. Stay the one good thing that Snowflake hadn’t ruined yet.

The day was getting brighter. Not warm yet—just less dark. The sky was turning from black to the color of a bruise that was finally, slowly beginning to heal.

Snowie stood up. She walked to Snowflake’s side and slid her shoulder under Snowflake’s armpit. Snowflake hesitated—just a second, a small stiffness, the body’s reflexive resistance to accepting help. Then she let her weight fall.

They walked. Slowly, clumsily, two figures supporting each other across the grey landscape, leaving a trail of wet footprints in the mud.

Snowflake often played here when she was little, but she had never been here before sunrise. The observation platform was a wooden deck jutting out over the valley. Below, the world was still shadows and outlines—the shapes of trees, the distant glint of the Stream, thin and cold as a vein. Above, the sky was beginning to thin.

They sat down in the mud near the edge. Snowie sat close. Not touching, but close enough that Snowflake could feel the faintest suggestion of warmth beside her—small, fragile, barely there, but real.

“So… have we watched the sunrise here before?”

Snowflake shook her head.

“Then what should we do? Are we gonna just wait?”

Snowflake didn’t answer. She looked down at the ground. The mud was soft, dark, and formless—the kind of surface that accepts anything you give it and holds the shape. She reached out and fetched a small limb from a dead branch lying nearby. Then she pressed it into the mud and drew.

“What is this? A potato?”

Snowflake actually wanted to draw the sun. But as she had said before, her drawing technique was better-than-nothing. The shape on the ground was lopsided, misshapen, vaguely round. A potato with aspirations.
She moved the stick lower and began to write. The letters were slow. Each one was a deliberate act. Not careful—deliberate. There was a difference. Careful meant afraid of making a mistake. Deliberate meant knowing exactly what you wanted to say and saying it with nothing in between. No fortress. No filter. No “Nothing happened.”

I love you

Her hoof suddenly shivered. The stick stopped. The period mark wasn’t inserted.

A drop of water fell on that spot. It hit the mud with a soft, final sound, appending the dot back for her.

It was Snowflake’s tears.

She had kept them for eight years.
