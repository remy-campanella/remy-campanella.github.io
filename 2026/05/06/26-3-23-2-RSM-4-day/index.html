【RSM】 Chapter 4 An Ordinary Day

发表于2026-05-06|更新于2026-05-30|艺术文学拙作Reminiscences de Snowflake Mapleleaf
|浏览量:

Hay Café.

The mixed smell of hay and sweet coffee was one of the characteristics of this store. The morning sunshine was lazily smeared on the glass panes of the window, softly wove with the chocolate-colored decoration. Along with potted plants growing inside and outside the store, the oil paint wouldn’t disappear until noon.

The waiter stallion was standing at the front desk as usual, welcoming guests and recommending dishes. When he was not busy, he would just lean at the front desk and glance at the ponies outside.

The disc store at the opposite direction updated their new albums, the poster on the door was about Crystalian post-punk.

The waiter slightly shook his head. He could nither speak nor understand Crystalian, but the powerful tempo and rebellious melody really has something.

His lips arched up. He couldn’t help recalling how exciting that Miss Kirin was when the genre was mentioned. She told him it’s okay not to fully understand the lyrics, as long as your heart echoed with the music.

“She actually likes that?” the waiter thought to himself, “Who knows?”

The waiter looked towards the seat beside the window. She was not there, and for several days. He wondered why she didn’t appear, because she had never left bad comments on meals.

“Cutie Mark Crusaders cupcakes hunting mission GO!”

Suddenly three little fillies dashed by the door, stirred up a gust of wind, and nearly ran into a chair.

“C’mon, the last one to get to the Sugarcube Corner is the chicken!” Scootaloo took the lead.

“It’s not fair!” Sweetie Belle tried her best to keep up.

“She’ll definitely be the chicken if she lost her scooter!” Apple Bloom was sweating bad.

Three ponies left the main road and turned into a shorter dirt path. Suddenly Scootaloo braked to stop and they smashed together.

“Get off from me, Scootaloo!”

“Unless you let go of my hooves!”

“Why did you stopped?” Apple Bloom asked, peeling herself off Scootaloo’s flank.

“I uh… something occurred to me.” Scootaloo looked on the left. Scootaloo looked to the left, her scooter’s wheel still spinning in the dirt. They’re passing by the backyard of the school. “We’ve always been doing our homework together, right?”

“Yeah? What’s the problem?”

“Have we finished our music ones?”

“What are you thinking about?” Sweetie Belle stood up from the ground. “The one who could finish Miss Snowflake’s homework hasn’t been born yet.”

“Please don’t let me think of this!” Apple Bloom covered her face with her hooves, “How could she turn music classes into the biggest boredom?”

“At least the dance is good,” Scootaloo helped up her little scooter, “but she finished it too fast, I wasn’t started having fun yet!”

“Then who’s fault it is?” Sweetie Belle asked.

“The last thing I want to do in the class is to be picked.” Apple Bloom said, “Even she’s actually not angry if I cannot answer.”

“But she did quite a lot for us, right?” Scootaloo said, “She wants us to understand, and her explanation did reduced some difficultes but… I just wish I could be smarter.”

“That’s not all.” Sweetie Belle continued, “Sometimes I woke up at midnight, I could see the lights in the attic were still on, literally EVERY time.”

“Girls, do you think this is okay?” Apple Bloom said, “I don’t dare to imagine what is it like as the class goes harder.”

“Well, worrying is useless.” Scootaloo got on her scooter, “Finish this mission, and we’ve got work to do.”

“All right Derpy, you got this.”

Derpy stopped in front of the door of the attic, blinked her eyes twice and pulled out a piece of wrinkled delivery list. She once again pull the paper closer to spell the customer’s name. “S-N-O-W-F-L-A-K-E! Right, here she is!”

Derpy still remembered the accident when she first met Snowflake. This is a good chance for her to save Snowflake’s impression on post office.

Knock, knock.

“Hello?” A kirin’s head reached out from the door’s crack.

“Good afternoon, Miss! I’m Derpy Hooves from Ponyville Post Office. Your package with last digits 04917 has arrived. Here is your package.”

Snowflake squinted her eyes. “Did I… bought anything?”

“Absolutely yes!” Derpy passed the package to Snowflake, “I checked the list once before leaving the office, once on the halfway, and once before I knocked on your door. Nothing could be wrong this time!”

Snowflake took the package. The squared-shaped package must be the novel The Romance of Lunagrad. She was so busy these days that she nearly forgot it.

“Oh, well, thank you.” Snowflake casually threw the novel on the sofa.

“Um… Aren’t you going to examine the goods?”

She has a point, Snowflake thought. She picked up the package and returned. Then she started rolling the package to find something.

“Miss, do you need a scissors to—”

Snowflake didn’t wait.

She drove her hooves into the gap between the cardboard flaps.

SNAP!

A sharp, brutal tear. The packaging split open.

Derpy flinched. She took a step back, her wings twitching.

Snowflake pulled out the book.

It was heavy. The cover was bound in deep midnight-blue leather, cold and smooth to the touch. On the front, a stylized golden crescent moon and several scattered stars were embossed, catching the dim light of the room. The gilt edges of the pages shimmered like a solid block of gold.

Romance of Lunagrad

It smelled of old paper and fresh ink. Snowflake ran her hoof over the spine, feeling the raised ribs of the binding. A pristine 18651 edition.

“Title, author, publisher, 1865 collector’s edition, 1907 reprinting… Yeah, that should be it.” Then Snowflake once again turned around and laid the book on the sofa.

“Wait! You forgot to sign on the receiept!” Derpy was nearly crying with her stomping hooves. She couldn’t bear another public criticism by the office manager for the ignored process. Snowflake was quite annoyed, but Derpy was just doing her job. She drew out the pen from Derpy’s pocket and signed her name. That might be the most illegible words she had ever written.

“Alright Miss, thank you for your cooperation. I’m always glad to be at your—”

Click.

The door latched shut, smoothly and efficiently, barely filled the position of a goodbye, leaving Derpy alone confused in the dark corridor. She did completed her task, but the customer didn’t seemed to be satisfied. Well, at least she won’t be faced with the anger from the manager.

“No, no, Angel, we’ve agreed earlier,” Fluttershy slightly shook her right hoof to get rid of the bunny’s detainment, “I’ll go to Snowflake’s and you’ll stay at home with Discord.”

Angel hung down his ears.

“Discord, come here.” Fluttershy called, and Discord popped out from the tea pot. “Yes, my dear?”

Fluttershy cleared her throat. “Tonight I’ll go out. And the task for you two is to stay home together, safe and sound. You hear?”

“Psst, I thought it could be harder.” Discord said. “I won’t appear in front of him so that he’ll never find an excuse to provoke me this time.”

“DON’T even think of that.” Fluttershy gazed at Discord. “It’s not your Guys’ Night and your job is to take care of Angel.”

“Uh, fine.” Discord sank back into the teapot. “But I won’t make tea.”

It didn’t take long for Fluttershy to reach the Central Music Hall. She didn’t go through the main gate, but took a detour around the building. As expected, Snowflake’s window was still lit. A shadow moved behind the curtain, back and forth, like a pendulum.

Fluttershy sighed.

Knock, knock.

The shadow stopped. Hoofsteps, slow and reluctant. The door opened a crack.

“Fluttershy?” Snowflake’s mane was looser than usual, strands falling across her face. “What is it?”

“Have you got some free time?” Fluttershy asked. “You know, for a friend’s visit?”

Snowflake glanced back. Fluttershy caught a glimpse of the desk behind her—buried under paper.

“Sorry. I’m kind of busy.”

“But you’ve worked all day.” Fluttershy didn’t move. “Don’t you feel tired?”

Snowflake’s hoof gripped the doorframe. She looked at Fluttershy. Then at the floor.

“Besides…” Fluttershy lifted a pink box. “I brought cupcakes.”

A pause.

Snowflake’s grip loosened.

“…Fine. Come in.”

“Sorry, sorry—” Snowflake was already clearing the sofa, shoving stacks of sheet music into a drawer. “I didn’t expect—the room isn’t—”

“Don’t worry.” Fluttershy sat down and opened the box. Twelve cupcakes, frosting in shades of pink and brown, arranged in neat rows. In this room full of paper and ink, they looked almost alien.

Snowflake gave up on cleaning and sat beside her.

“What are you busy with these days?”

“Checking homework.”

“Must be fulfilling. Your knowledge finally has somewhere to go.”

Snowflake picked up a strawberry cupcake. Turned it over. “It’s all right.”

“I sometimes think about the time I visited the kirins,” Fluttershy said. “The jungle, the hot springs… oh, and Autumn Blaze. She’s so talkative.”

A sound escaped Snowflake’s nose. Not quite a laugh.

“Back then, four fifths of my social life was her.”

“You two know each other?”

“Schoolmates. She was in the College of Literature.”

“Considering her imagination, no wonder. What’s she doing now?”

“Writer. Journalist to pay the bills.” Snowflake took a bite of the cupcake. “She mails me her poems sometimes.”

“What a friendship between two artists.”

“I wouldn’t call myself an artist.” Snowflake stared at the frosting on her hoof. “I just worked hard. That’s all.”

“I can see that.” Fluttershy’s eyes drifted to the paper mountain on the desk. “And you brought that habit here.”

Snowflake didn’t answer.

“If I could ask,” Fluttershy said softly, “what made you come all the way here? To be a teacher?”

Snowflake’s chewing slowed. She looked at the cupcake. At the desk. At the window, where the night was pressing against the glass.

“Just a decision.”

Fluttershy nodded. The room settled into silence.

“Oh, before I forget.” Fluttershy reached into her saddlebag and pulled out a folded piece of paper. “Twilight wanted me to give you this.”

Snowflake unfolded it.

Dear Snowflake,

I hope this letter finds you well and that your lesson planning is proceeding smoothly.

During my recent correspondence with Princess Luna, I happened to mention that you are teaching here in Ponyville. It occurred to her that, despite her long years, she has never actually had the pleasure of meeting a kirin in person. She is quite fascinated by your kind’s history and culture, and expressed a strong interest in finally having the chance to speak with one.

Fortunately, Princess Luna’s upcoming southern tour includes a stop in Ponyville this Friday. I have arranged a small, private dinner at the Castle of Friendship so we can all chat in a relaxed setting.

  • Time: This Friday, 7:00 PM
  • Location: The West Wing Dining Room, Castle of Friendship
  • Attendees: Princess Luna, myself, and you.

Please don’t feel the need to prepare anything formal—this is simply an evening for good food and pleasant conversation among friends.

I look forward to seeing you there.

Warmly,

Twilight Sparkle

Snowflake read it. Once. Then again.

She set the paper on the table. Smoothed a crease. Smoothed it again.

“Why?” Fluttershy tilted her head. “Aren’t you happy? Even I don’t get to have dinner with Princess Luna that often.”

Snowflake stood up. She walked to the window, then to the desk, then back to the window. Her hoof tapped the glass, once, twice.

The letter sat on the table. Princess Luna. Castle of Friendship. This Friday.

“Fine.” Snowflake turned around. “I’ll be there.”

“Great!” Fluttershy stood up, cleaning crumbs from her body. “I’ll let Twilight know on my way home.”

“Alright.” Snowflake walked her to the door. “Thank you. For the cupcakes.”

“Anytime.” Fluttershy paused at the threshold. She looked back.

“Don’t be nervous. Just act like it’s one of our picnics.”

Snowflake’s mouth opened.

Then closed.

She didn’t say anything. She just offered a small, stiff nod.

Fluttershy smiled and stepped out.

The door closed. Snowflake stood in the hallway, listening to the fading sound of hooves on the path outside.

She walked back to the table. The letter was still there, crisp and white against the chaos.

She didn’t pick it up again.

She sat down, pulled a stack of homework toward her, and picked up her pen.

The pen hovered over the paper.

It didn’t move for a long time.
