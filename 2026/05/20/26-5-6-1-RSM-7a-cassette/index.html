【RSM】 Interval 1 Cassette

发表于2026-05-20|更新于2026-05-20|艺术文学拙作Reminiscences de Snowflake Mapleleaf
|浏览量:

The clock ticked on the wall.

The door was clicked open when the clock pointed at 7:00 PM. There walked in a middle-aged male kirin, with his white fur was spotted with dust, and his hair was slightly messed up by the frustration.

“Home?”

“Uh huh.”

There was a female kirin busying in the kitchen. She was in a white checkered apron around her pink body, and a color-faded yellow hair band tied up her red mane. The oil was bubbling in the heating pan, as the female kirin collecting cut cabbage from the chopping board. The moment she thought the oil pan was ready, she quickly threw the cabbage into the pan. The sizzling sound spread over the whole house. She smelled the fume.

Ah— CHOO!

“Oh dear, is our daughter missing you again?” Westwind said with a light smile. He drew out a half-full wine bottle and pulled out the cork, then poured out a half cup of the wine.

“Just say it straight if you’re jealous.” Crimson gave a glance and turned on the kitchen hood, the spatula in her another hoof kept stirring.

“Snowie’s pet name came from you,” Crimson said, still facing the pan, “but you had never called her Snowie later on.”

Westwind’s cup paused beside his mouth, then he took a sip of the wine.

The sun was getting lower, the light came through the window, projecting a golden square right on the dining table against the wall. Westwind sat on the left while Crimson sat on the right after she placed the cabbage plate on the table. Along with the stewed fish finished before, that’s two dishes for the couple’s dinner.

“Hmm. Finally, you accepted my suggestion and added enough salt.” Westwind said after tasted the fish.

“Just don’t be regret for your blood pressure when you get old.” Crimson picked him a piece of cabbage stem, “Eat more vegetables, Windy, or you’ll cry on the weighing scale.”

“Hey, I’m already 56 years old, no male could avoid weight.”

“But you have to work 10 years more to retire,” Crimson giggled, “you’re still a young fellow after all.”

“Which… means… I don’t need to worry about blood pressure?”

“Oh hush you!” Crimson said in a slightly angry tone, but her smile still remained, “Or I’ll let Snowie know her father is setting himself free again.”

“Alright, you win.”

The sun went down behind the mountains, the couple had finished their dinner. Westwind sometimes looked at the streetlamps outside, sometimes looked at Crimson doing the dishes.

“Have you got in touch with Snowie?” Crimson asked.

“I phoned her five times,” Westwind answered, “and in the sixth time she said she was in class.”

“Sounds like quite busy.” Crimson started wiping plates, “How does she feel?”

“I don’t know,” Westwind started peeling an orange, “she said she was fine, then ten minutes later she said she was about to enter the library, and she hung up the phone.”

“The same thing happened when I called her when she was in the university.” Crimson sighed, “Anyway, how’s your work today?”

“Nothing.” Westwind replied, “Our team leader didn’t assigned me new tasks, and you know I can’t use my phone in the office. I was so bored that I started cleaning up my workstation.”

“That’s very rare for you. You’d never helped me cleaning the house.”

“Ha, whatever you say.” Westwind walked towards his bag, “But I did find something.”

Crimson followed out, with a piece of rag drying off her hooves. She leaned her head against Westwind’s shoulder and looked inside: it was a case of cassette, with its shell and tag had both worn old, and the printing and handwriting had faded through the time.

“A cassette? When did you buy it?” Crimson asked.

“Quite long ago, I guess.” Westwind checked the cassette over and over. “Maybe… our wedding time?”

“How could we have enough time to care about a cassette then?” Crimson said, “You actually bought it at our last date before our marriage, you wanted to impress me by looking more… ‘cultured’.”

“But I AM cultured.”

“So it worked, didn’t it?” Crimson giggled, “Where is the tape recorder?”

“I think it’s in her room.” Westwind looked through the corridor, “You know, she used that to learn Ponish?”

Unlike other teenagers, Snowflake’s room was at the end of the corridor, the wooden door just stood there without any sign of personality. You would just missed it when you walked by.

Westwind rotated the cold metal handle and pushed the door open. The movement stirred up the dust on the floor, brought out the path of the sunshine passed through the half-curtained window. The air in the room which hadn’t moved since Snowflake’s left blew on their faces, bringing the smell of wooden furniture.

“I should have cleaned this room more often.” Crimson covered her nose with her hoof, “Okay, where is it?”

Westwind bent in front of Snowflake’s desk. He pulled open the drawer at the bottom and placed it on the piano. Then he touched and found the socket on the wall and inserted the plug into it. The buzzer left out a hum reluctantly, and the nixie tubes were blinkingly lit.

Westwind open the box and slotted the cassette in, then he pushed the play button.

The machine started running, the parts moved one by one, then the spools caught, and the tape began to move.

A guitar strummed. Then a voice—deep, measured, singing in languages they had listened for years. The melody was slow and deliberate, the kind of folk song that older generations sang at gatherings when the wine had been poured and the children were asleep. The sound was thin and slightly wobbly, textured with the hiss and pop of aged magnetic tape, like listening to a memory through a wall.

Crimson sat on the piano stool, with her chin resting on her hoof. She listened for a moment, then her mouth curled.

“You really are an old gentleman.” Crimson said, “The song had already been out of style even in the time of our wedding.”

“But you love it too, don’t you?” Westwind replied, “Or you wouldn’t let me buy it.”

Click. The cassette reached the end. The machine stopped.

But the music sounded like still spinning around the ceiling lamp wire.

“Ah, old songs,” Crimson closed her eyes, “always better when older.”

“Same with the wine.”

“The only thing in your head?”

Westwind giggled softly. He opened the box and flipped the side if the cassette.

After the same hisses and pops, the recorder stayed silent with the background noise. Then the intro started in a agile piano melody, simple enough, but still mingled with some wrong notes.

Then a sweet little girl’s voice, followed after a break.

Железный шлем, деревянный костыль. Король с войны возвращался домой.
An iron helmet, a wooden pole. The king returned home from war.
Солдаты пели, глотая пыль. И пел с ними вместе король хромой.1
Soldiers sang, swallowing dust. And the hunchbacked king sang with them.

It was sang in Crystalian. The words were soft yet bright, the vowels stretched in the way children stretch them when they haven’t yet learned where syllables end. The melody was simple—a rhythmic tune, the kind that children sing while playing. The pronunciation was imperfect. Some of the trills were blurred. But the voice itself was clear, the way only a child’s voice can be clear, untouched by the world’s roughness, as if sound could be made of glass.

Westwind and Crimson looked at each other?

“I’m pretty sure this cassette is about Ponish songs.” Westwind scratched the back of his head. “What happened?”

“An old cassette, a children’s voice, a piano accompaniment, a language we would never understand,” Crimson couldn’t hold back her smile, “don’t tell me… Snowie did this?”

Троянский бархат, немурский шелк. На башне ждала королева, и вот.
Trojan velvet, Nemur’s silk. The queen waited on the tower, and now.
Платком она машет, завидев полк. Она смеется, она поет.
She waves her handkerchief at the sight of the regiment. She laughs, she sings.

“When? How did I don’t know?” Westwind just couldn’t believe it, like he had missed a big event.

“You were too busy, don’t you remember?” Crimson crossed her hooves against her chest, “You were always on a business trip, seldom answering my calls.” Something suddenly hit her, “Hey, maybe Snowie learned this from you!”

“I called you back every time.”

“But she learned she could just easily decline other’s care.”

Рваная обувь, а в шляпе цветок. Плясал на площади люд простой…
Ragged shoes, and a flower on the hat. He danced on the square with common folk…
Он тоже пел, он молчать не мог. В такую минуту и в день такой.
He sang too, he couldn’t remain silent. In such a moment, in such a day.

The music went on. The melody was still welcoming, the lyrics was still incomprehensible, but Westwind and Crimson travelled years ago, when they were still lovers, when their daughter said the first word, when Snowie played the first note.

The music ended with a short cadence. There was a pause on the tape—a hiss of dead air—and then the child’s voice again, unprompted, speaking to no one in particular:

“Mama, I’m finished!”

A tiny laugh. The sound of hooves scrambling against the piano bench. And then the tape ran out of recorded material, leaving only the soft mechanical hum of the player.

Westwind and Crimson sat without speaking.

Crimson’s eyes were on the cassette player, but she was looking at something far beyond it. Her hoof had found its way to the edge of the table and was resting there, motionless, beside Westwind’s half-peeled orange.

“She was—”“Westwind started.

“Nine.” Crimson said quietly. “Maybe ten. She had started learning Crystalian for a while. She found a Crystal folk song in one of my dad’s old treasure and insisted on singing it. Over and over. I think she took the wrong cassette.” A pause. “She was so happy.”

Westwind looked at the orange in his hoof. He set it down.

“I forgot this was on there.” he said.

“So did I.”

The cassette player hummed. Neither of them moved to turn it off.

Click.

The hum stopped. The kitchen was quiet. The refrigerator hummed. A bird chirped to return the nest. Not far from the house, a door closed.

Crimson ejected the cassette and slid it back into its cracked case. She ran her hoof across the yellowed label, tracing the faded blue letters without reading them.

“Put it somewhere safe,” she said. “Don’t lose it.”

Westwind took the case from her. He held it for a moment—lightly, the way one holds a small, living thing—then stood up and walked to the cabinet by the hallway. He opened the top shelf, moved a box of candles aside, and placed the cassette case behind it. Safe from damp. Safe from being forgotten again. Safe from being played again.

He closed the cabinet door.

Outside, the last trace of daylight had vanished. The window above the kitchen sink reflected nothing now—only the warm yellow light of the kitchen itself, a small bright square in the dark.

Crimson picked up the dishrag again.

“I’ll make tea,” Westwind said.

The water boiled. The tea steeped. They sat at the table—Crimson on the right, Westwind on the left—and drank without talking. The orange lay half-peeled between them, slowly drying at the edges.

Бой барабанный, знамен карнавал. Король с войны возвратился домой.
The drum has beaten, the carnival’s banner. The king has returned home from war.
Войну проиграл, полноги потерял. Но рад был до слез, что остался живой.
He lost the war, he lost his wealth, But he was joyful to the tears, that he had survived.

  1. The lyrics in this chapter is from the Russian song Хромой король(The Lame King). The Lame King is a song by Alexander Dulov based on the composition of Maurice Carême Le retour du roi, translated from French by Mikhail Kudinov. The song exists not only in Russian, but also in French, German, Esperanto. The song was repeatedly appear in the repertoire of professional pop performers and records.
