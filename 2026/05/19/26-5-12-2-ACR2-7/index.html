The deadline of the expository essay: 11pm, Sunday, June 21nd.

Warmer

  • What is a culture shock?
    Culture shock is a kind of experience when discovering different culture for the observer’s.

About Expository Essay

  1. How many words should you write?

    You are required to write a 1000–1200-word (+/-10%) essay by responding to a given essay question.

  2. How many in-text citations should you write?

    Use correctly formatted in-text citations to acknowledge information from your researched sources. Provide 6 to 8 citations throughout the essay.

  3. How many sources should you use?

    Find 6 to 8 sources which you will use to write the essay.

  4. Can you use ChatGPT in your essay?

    AI generators can help you in a number of ways: translation, defining words or producing synonyms etc. You cannot ask an AI generator to produce an entire essay, or any sections of an essay and submit that as part of your assessment.

  5. Can you write about any topic?

    You can choose possible research topics for your essay but not limited to recommended topics.

  6. Should you use citations for a paraphrased idea?

    You must use citations for both direct quotes and paraphrased ideas from researched sources. A reference list is required for this task.

Recommended Topics

Prompt: Examine and discuss what a student would expect to encounter in terms of [YOUR CHOSEN TOPIC] when moving to [YOUR CHOSEN FOREIGN COUNTRY] for university.

Given word limits and time restraints of the assessment, it would be impossible to cover all aspects of the prompt. You can choose possible research topics for your essay but not limited to these topics:

  • Food and eating practices (e.g., common or unfamiliar foods, eating habits, food culture)
  • Climate and weather (e.g., seasonal weather patterns, extreme weather, climate differences)
  • Library services and procedures (e.g., borrowing systems, study spaces, digital resources)
  • University/academic student services (e.g., academic support, counselling, career services, university unions)
  • Local culture/customs (e.g., social norms, traditions, everyday behaviour)
  • Accommodation (e.g., on-campus housing, private rentals, shared living, homestays)
  • Transportation (e.g., public transport systems, commuting, travel costs)
  • Public/university holidays (e.g., national holidays, academic calendars)
  • Part-time work (e.g., student employment options, workplace culture, work/study balance)
  • History (e.g., historical events, local history, national history)
  • Accents/language use/idioms (e.g., pronunciation differences across regions, slang, everyday expressions)
