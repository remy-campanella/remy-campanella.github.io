【RSM】 Chapter 3 Picnic Tones

发表于2026-04-11|更新于2026-04-11|艺术文学拙作Reminiscences de Snowflake Mapleleaf
|浏览量:

“What do you call a mosquito?”

“Um… what is it?”

“We don’t call a mosquito, they just come and bite.”

“Hahahaha…”

“I knew I should invite you here.” Pinkie Pie hugged Snowflake’s shoulder. “Best picnic EVER!”

“It’s been a long time since we’ve invited others to join.” Rarity crouched on her sofa, gracefully passed a cup of tea with her magic. “How do you feel?”

“Thank you, far better than ever.” Snowflake took over the cup. “I mean, the last time I went on a picnic might be the spring break when I was in primary school.”

It was a fine Sunday, a perfect weekend break for working ponies. Pinkie Pie chose a hill top in Sweet Apple Acres, from which they can have a birdview of Ponyville: two mountain ranges were enclosing a flat area, with River Saddle crossing from the north to the south. From the east bank where Sweet Apple Acres and their picnic spot was, the town structure was clearly in the panoramic view.

Snowflake didn’t have that luck to enjoy the scenery right now. During previous weeks of the work, her life was limited to three parts: teaching, researching, and going to bed. She might still be in the library of Friendship School now if Pinkie’s invitation didn’t broke in last night. Now, she is brainstorming about possible questions that others may ask.

“Tourist guides have a high evaluation on Kirin Village’s scenery.” Twilight turned a page of her book. “How is it actually?”

“Any kind of scenery would be boring if you were born there.” Snowflake took a sip of her tea. “Apart from the mountain pass where Autumn Blaze lives, I don’t know where else is worth for me to see.”

“Take it for granted, huh?” Applejack said while chewing an apple. “Things could be worse if you’re still silent.”

“Now you need to keep it cool from the niriks.” Fluttershy grinned.

“Oh, oh, can you turn into a nirik now?” Rainbow Dash dashed in front of Snowflake’s face. “That would be SO COOL!”

“Sorry Dash.” Snowflake gently pushed away Rainbow Dash’s nose. “It’s dangerous, not cool. I need to be too angry to lost my mind to do that.”

“Did someone say ‘lost my mind’?”

Snowflake stared in fright at the picnic basket beside her. She swore she never left this basket alone it had never talked.

“Quit it, Discord.” Fluttershy sighed. “It’s not polite to play pranks on our new friend.”

“Is it?” After a short snap, the weirdest figure that Snowflake had ever seen rose in front of her: a mixture of an eagle claw, a lion paw, a snake tail and a dragon head… Seems there weren’t any pair of two parts comes from the same species.

“You’d rather call an unacquainted kirin than me.” Discord protested. “You, the Elements of Harmony, in turn require me of politeness.”

“Sorry about the offence, Snowflake.” Fluttershy walked towards and blocked in the middle of them. “I hope you won’t mind a little randomness from Discord.”

“We’ll see…” Discord cunningly smiled.

“What do you want Discord?” Rainbow Dash couldn’t bear his face for a single second.

“Angel won’t let me to take his blanket to wash.” Discord complained. “Seriously, what makes him think I can’t teach him a lesson?”

“Ugh, easy Discord,” Fluttershy covered her face with her hooves. It’s not the first time that Discord interrupts her enjoyment. “You’re just too impatient while being with animals. Take it slow, watch your pace, lower your volume, always works.”

“Psst, sounds like a music teacher.”

“Here! Here! We’ve got a music teacher right HERE!” Pinkie Pie pushed Snowflake towards Discord, “Come on, Snowflake, teach him a LESSON!”

Pinkie! What are you doing? That’s totally not on my plan!

“I… Uh…” Snowflake was stuck with an awkward face.

“Nah, a teacher cannot teach without proper facilities!” Discord said and did a magic snap. Suddenly, there flashed out a desk and a chair, Discord sat on the chair and shrinked himself into a school-age pony’s size.

Snowflake winked rapidly to seek for help from Fluttershy, but Fluttershy seemed not getting the point. “You forgot something for your teacher.”

“Oh, right. Look at me, never been a respectful student.” Discord laughed. The second snap brought a blackboard and a box of chalk on Snowflake’s side. “What about now?”

“Ahhh! They bite!” Snowflake frightenedly scrambled back, but that didn’t stop Discord’s chalks from climbing and biting on her fur and mane.

“Is this what ‘respectful’ means in your dictionary?” Twilight shook her head, interrupted Discord’s magic. Then she casted a teleport spell on Snowflake’s hoof. “Here, use this. I bought this box of chalk from Quilts and Sofas, always reliable.”

“Thank you.” Snowflake pulled out a white one. “What do you want to learn?”

“Something ‘discord’!”

Discord suddenly leaned forward, his lion paw supporting his chin, his eyes twinkling with mischief. “You know, something that sounds absolutely horrible? Discordious? Chaotic? Something that would make Rarity’s ears bleed?”

Snowflake blinked. She hadn’t prepared for this specific question. Her mind raced through her carefully organized notes, searching for a keyword. “Discord…? Well, in academic terms, we don’t usually define it as ‘discord’, but rather as… ‘dissonant’ or ‘atonal’.”

“Atonal!” Discord clapped his eagle claw and lion paw together. “Sounds delightful! Tell me more.”

“Well…” Snowflake cleared her throat, gripping the reliable white chalk tightly. “If you are looking for a systematic method to… uh, destroy the traditional sense of harmony, there is a technique called the Twelve-Tone Technique.”

“Twelve tones? I have more than twelve tones in my toe, but go on.”

“Actually, it’s about equality,” Snowflake started, her voice shaking slightly but gaining momentum as she entered the familiar territory of theory. “In traditional tonal music, there is a hierarchy. A ‘King’ note—the Tonic—and its subjects. But in Twelve-Tone Serialism, created by Arnold Schönberg…”

She quickly drew a grid on the blackboard, writing 12 notes from C to B in a row.

“…every single one of the twelve pitches of the chromatic scale must be treated equally. You must create a Tone Row, a specific order of these twelve notes, and no note can be repeated until the other eleven have been played.”

“No repeats?” Discord gasped dramatically. “How wonderfully obsessive!”

“It is not obsessive, it is structural integrity!” Snowflake corrected him instinctively, “It ensures the absence of a tonic. No key center. No ‘home’. It means… you are always moving, never resolving. It is mathematically designed to avoid the expectation of consonance.”

She drew a bracket over the scale.

“In short, it is the rigorous avoidance of order—through a very, very strict order. The logic is absolute, even if the sound is… well, to some ears…”

“Painful?” Discord offered, grinning.

“Complex!” Snowflake insisted, sweating a little. “It is the sound of logic refusing to bow to beauty.”

“Magnificent!” Discord jumped up from his desk, his small pony body morphing back into his chaotic self instantly. “A music that defies the tyrant of ‘sounding good’! A music of pure, unbridled anarchy, wrapped in a boring little rulebook!”

He snapped his fingers. Suddenly, the picnic blanket stood up on two legs and started tap-dancing, while the apples from Applejack’s basket floated into the air, arranging themselves into a twelve-tone row that hummed a dissonant, clashing melody.

Hide your sword now wounded↑ knight↑~~,
your vain glor~ious gasconnade↓~~!
Brought you to↑ your final fight↑,
for your pride, high price↑ you’ve paid↓~!1

“Look at me, Snowflake! I’m a Tone Row!” Discord laughed, spinning around. “No home! No king! Just… me, myself, and my twelve chaotic friends!”

Snowflake put down the chalk, her face pale. “That… was not exactly what the textbook intended…”

What did Snowflake learn from the picnic? Well, don’t try to do hasty guess about nature of your students. She felt she must be polluted by Discord’s phantom, because she tried to think about her class preparation, but her head was always seized by Discord’s opera.

“Maybe I really need a library time right now.”

Snowflake returned to her attic and fetched her books, then she quickly went to Friendship School’s library. Instead of sitting at the table near the window, she chose the smallest table that others would hardly notice. Seeking loneliness in a public library, her choice has always been so contradicting.

She wrote a title on her notebook:

Lesson 4 Scales and Modes

Snowflake’s head started aching once she saw that title. She was afraid to imagine how awful it was to help students to remember 24 modes. In fact, when she was a student, she rote memorized them and reinforced that memory by mass practice. She didn’t want her students to do the same, but were there any choices?

No time for worrying now, because she felt someone was watching her.

“Good afternoon, dear.” Miss Cheerilee sat in the opposite side, handling a novel.

“Oh, hi Miss, what a coincidence.” Snowflake replied.

“Is that a treble clef?” Miss Cheerilee’s sight moved on Snowflake’s notebook, “Are you preparing for the next class?”

“Yes, they’re gonna learn 24 modes tomorrow.” Snowflake opened the textbook. “And I’m working on how to help them memorize that.”

Instead of going on, Miss Cheerilee picked up Snowflake’s notebook and turned it forward. “Wow, you’ve prepared so much.” Miss Cheerilee said, “It’s like… nearly 15 pages for a single lesson.”

“I spent three classes to teach them syncopated rhythm,” Snowflake sighed, “they just struggle to accept theories even with a little abstraction.”

“So that’s why you put so much work to yourself?” Cheerilee turned the pages, lokking confused.

“Well, I don’t think that’s ‘much’, it’s relevant.” Snowflake got her notebook back, “If I could do more research, like looking up their old textbook, I would discover those challenges in advance.”

“Uhm, that makes sense.” Cheerilee nodded, “Well, lucky we are in a library. We don’t need to come up with a new solution for such a hot topic.”

……

“Ugh, who published this book?” Miss Cheerilee was struggling under the weight of a large book on her back. Fundamentals of Dragon Music was at least two times wider than her body, with its both cover engraved with scale-like sheetmetal. “Can’t they just use standard book bindings?”

Snowflake was amused by her exhaustion. “You know, dragons are big, dragons’ place is hot. They have to use bigger and inflammable material.”

“Never mind.” Cheerilee leaned her back to let the heavy book slide to the table. “Now let’s see what have they got.”

Dragon’s book has probably the best durability in the world. If the two teachers weren’t in the library, others might think they’re repeatedly opening heavy trapdoors: pages were made of thick paper as hard as wood, and bound with rings and nails. Turning a single page needs both of them working together.

“F… Finally.” Snowflake was gasping deeply, with sweat all over her head. “Does it say anything?”

“Books don’t talk, silly.” Cheerilee grinned. “It mentioned a method using knuckles and space between them.”

“Heh, you think we could use that?” Snowflake lowered her head and looked at her round hoof.

“Well I guess not.” Cheerilee closed the book.

“Maybe we need to look up works of ponies first.” Snowflake moved the book against the table. “Did you find any?”

“Well, Beginning Theory for Fillies, does this help?” Cheerilee passed a small pink book.

“Wow, they really dare let six-year-old children learn 24 modes.” Snowflake pointed at the page. “They’ve woven the patterns into dance rhythms.”

“Sounds very suitable for them.” Cheerilee said. “If you turned your back to the students for five minutes, I bet they could dance on the rooftop.”

“Hehe, sure did.” Snowflake copied the dance steps on her notebook.

“Okay, what’s next?”

“The next one is Dorian mode.” Snowflake kept writing. “You know, the major mode starts from C, minor mode starts from A, and Dorian mode starts from D. This mode is mainly named after by a tribe called Doria in Acient Griffinstone. Speaking of which, for the similar reason, major mode is previously called ‘Ionian mode’, minor mode is called ‘Aeolian’. But the Locrian mode isn’t, the name comes from the middle ages, it’s because…”

“Um, I thought there were only 24 modes in the textbook.” Cheerilee cannot but to interrupt her.

“Yes, well, a little expansion wouldn’t bother.” Snowflake replied.

“So you added Dorian, Ionian, Aeolian and… overtone inference?”

“Exactly! I want them know that music is color they could taste, not just marks on the staffs. There is so much more beauty beyond textbooks, they shouldn’t be trapped by those exams… like I was.”

“I understand that, dear.” Cheerilee’s tone softened. “But how long did you assigned for these?”

“Uh… Five, five, five… 20 minutes?” Snowflake counted.

“Your class only lasts for 40 minutes, Miss.” Cheerilee truned the notebook pages back to the dances. “Even if the finishing bell hasn’t rung, their focus will have drifted away automatically.”

“I… I…” Snowflake’s hoof stopped half-way.

“Besides, their cognitive load is limited, you can’t push those topics to them and let them learn in such a short time.” Cheerilee continued.

“I… I could do more, find easier ways to help them understand, that’s what a teacher should do, right?”

“No, you couldn’t.” Cheerilee’s words were still soft yet firm. “I’m not talking against your ideal, Snowflake. But the criterion of a good class is students’ experience.”

Snowflake was silent, with her eyes staring at the textbook.

“Here.” Cheerilee pointed at the 24 staffs on the page. “The exams only require them to remember the marks of 24 modes, nothing more, nothing less, they don’t need to know the alias and the frequency. The more you want to teach all of them, the more they might lost interest because the can’t keep up with you.”

Snowflake was still silent, but her actions were obedient. She picked up the pen, stroking the expansions one by one.

“There, all better.” Cheerilee cheerfully look at the reduced note. “Things has been a lot easier. All you need to do is to dance with them and let them practice.”

“Yeah… hehe… good…” Snowflake pretended a smile.

Night fell all over Ponyville. The sunny weather brought a star-shining sky. Most of the ponies were asleep, with their lights all turned off, wandering in their dreams.

Moonlight shone through the gap of Snowflake’s curtain, projected a cold line on the ground. Tick, tock, the room was silent except the clock sound slicing the time, like an uncared metronome. Snowflake was lying on her bed, rolling left and right like a dice.

Today was an exhausting day for Snowflake, mostly because of the chaotic picnic and the intense “surgical operation” on her lesson plan. She should have been drifted into a dreamless slumber the moment her head touched the pillow. Yet, sleep played a game of hide-and-seek with her.

Every time she closed her eyes, the rhythmic “tick-tock” of the clock seemed to morph into Discord’s tap-dancing beat, or the scratching sound of her chalk on the blackboard. That “opera”—if one could call that chaotic performance an opera—refused to leave her mind.

But it wasn’t just the noise. It was the interpretation.

She recalled Discord’s gleeful face as he sang that bizarre melody. “A music of pure, unbridled anarchy, wrapped in a boring little rulebook!” That was what he had called it. He had twisted the rigorous, mathematical order of the Twelve-Tone Technique into a joke.

Well, Twelve-tone is actually do joking enough compared to normal music. But the melody he sang was weird simply because the semitones and tritones. That’s not the excuse for Discord still stayed in stereotypes after she had explained so much.

Snowflake sat up in bed, the duvet sliding off her back. The moonlight illuminated her fretful face.

On the contrary, Discord is literally the lord of Chaos, if anything chaotic was discovered by academic world, then it must had already been Discord’s leftovers.

Then why he pretended to be a naïve pupil at first, then put a joke on her at the end?

Maybe… Discord did remained some expectation on her, but she was far from prepared to put on a fair class, so that he had to joking for a pleasing end?

Her gaze moved to the nightstand. There laid the notebook, significantly thinner than it had been that afternoon. Cheerilee’s words echoed in the silence: “The exams only require them to remember the marks of 24 modes… nothing more, nothing less.”

Snowflake stared at the notebook’s cover. The removal had been successful. The lesson plan was now safe. It was practical, fitting the time limit, and aligning with the exam standards.

But the empty feeling in her chest suggested that something vital had been amputated along with the “excess”. The history of the Dorian tribe, the physics of the overtone series—these were not just trivia. They were the color and the context that turned black notes on a staff into a living language. Without them, music class was just… memorization.

“Cheerilee is an experienced teacher,” Snowflake whispered to the silent room, trying to convince herself. “She knows the students’ limits. She knows what works. I can’t overwhelm them.”

But then, another thought surfaced, stubborn and sharp. Cheerilee taught general subjects, her task in music class was only being happy with kids. She didn’t major in music theory. She didn’t know how dry and suffocating a music class could become if stripped of its magic. Snowflake remembered her own school days, sitting in stiff chairs, reciting intervals like a robot, with no idea why they sounded the way they did. She had survived those classes, but she hadn’t lived in them until she discovered the “extra” knowledge on her own.

Did she want her students to merely survive her class? Or did she want to spark that same curiosity in them?

“Their curiosity is more important,” she murmured, her green eyes catching the moonlight. “If I don’t show them the horizon, they might never look up from the map.”

The conflict raged within her. On one side stood the obedient teacher who wanted to follow the rules and avoid failure; on the other stood the musician who feared that efficiency would kill the art.

Finally, she made her decision.

With a soft sigh that was more determination than resignation, Snowflake reached out and clicked on her bedside lamp. The warm yellow light pushed back the cold blue shadows of the night. She picked up her pen, the grip tight and familiar.

She opened the notebook to the clean, sparse lines Cheerilee had left behind.

“If I can explain it better,” Snowflake thought, a new energy fueling her tired limbs. “If I can weave the history into the dance… if I can make the physics a game rather than a lecture… maybe, just maybe, I can have both.”

She began to write. Slowly at first, then faster.

  1. Dorian

    • Origin: Ancient Griffinstone (Doria tribe).
    • Character: “Spartan” - Serious, solid, soldier-like.
    • Sound: Minor but raised 6th. “Hope in sadness.”

  2. Locrian

    • Status: “The Devil in music” (historical).
    • Structure: Unstable. No perfect cadence.
    • Analogy: A house with no front door.

She paused, tapping the pen against her chin. The “Overtone Inference” section had been the first to go under Cheerilee’s knife, deemed too mathematical for a music lesson. But Snowflake bit her lip.

“Cheerilee teaches maths, and she does it quite well,” she thought. “If I structure it correctly, the fillies already have the arithmetic skills to handle the logic. Deriving the series isn’t just calculation; it’s seeing the skeleton of sound itself.”

With a renewed spark of defiance, she turned to a fresh page and began to outline the derivation, keeping the language simple but the logic intact.

  1. The Mathematics of Nature (Overtone Inference)

    • Concept: Why do we have 12 notes? Science, not random choice.

    • Method: Use integer multiplication (Review: \(\times 2\), \(\times 3\), \(\times 4\)…).

    • Step 1: Fundamental Tone \(= f\) (Let \(f =\) C).

    • Step 2: The Harmonic Ladder (Calculate relative frequencies):

      • \(1f\) — C1 (The Root)
      • \(2f\) — C (Octave - Same note, higher)
      • \(3f\) — G (Perfect Fifth - The first new color!)
      • \(4f\) — c (Octave)
      • \(5f\) — e (Major Third - Now we have a chord!)

She didn’t just paste the text back in; she reworked it. She trimmed the jargon but kept the essence. She designed questions that led the students to discover the logic themselves, rather than being told.

Time seemed to dissolve around her. The ticking of the clock faded away, replaced by the scratching of ink on paper. She was in her element now, a conductor arranging a score, ensuring every note served a purpose.

It wasn’t until the faint chirping of the earliest birds broke the silence that Snowflake finally capped her pen. The clock on the wall showed 2:00 AM.

Her eyes burned, gritty and dry. Her neck ached from hunching over the notebook for hours. The fatigue she had dodged earlier finally crashed down on her like a tidal wave.

She looked at the notebook. It was thick again, bursting with neat handwriting, diagrams, and little musical annotations. It was a compromise—a dangerous, ambitious attempt to bridge the gap between the exam and the art. It was risky, heavy, but abundant asshe had expected.

But as Snowflake slumped back onto her pillow, a small, satisfied smile tugged at the corner of her mouth. Her heart was no longer empty; it was full.

“Just a few hours of sleep,” she mumbled, her eyelids fluttering shut. “Tomorrow… I have a class to teach.”
