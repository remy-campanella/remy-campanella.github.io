From Probabilistic Belief to Action

Classical Planning and Planning with Uncertainty

Classical planning Planning with uncertainty
Actions are deterministic. Actions may have stochastic outcomes.
The current state is known. Information may be imperfect.
A plan is expected to reach a goal. The goal may fail or be partially achieved.
Search checks reachability. The agent evaluates desirability of outcomes.
A goal condition says what must become true. A utility model says how good each outcome is.

Prediction vs Decision

Task Question
Prediction What may happen?
Decision What should the agent do?

Actions, Outcomes, and Preferences

State
A state of the world describes what actually happens, possibly hidden or uncertain to the agent.
Action
An action is a choice available to the agent.
Outcome
An outcome is the result produced by an action under a particular state of the world.

Utility

Utility
A utility is a numerical representation of how desirable an outcome is to an agent. Higher utility means the outcome is preferred.

Worked Example: Umbrella Decision

\[ P(Rain) = 0.4, P(NoRain) = 0.6 \]

Action \(Rain\) \(NoRain\)
\(TakeUmbrella\) \(70\) \(90\)
\(LeaveUmbrella\) \(0\) \(100\)
Expected Utility:
The expected utility of an action is the probability-weighted average utility of its possible outcomes:
\[ EU(a) = \sum_{s \in S}P(s)U(a, s) \]
  • \(a\): action
  • \(s\): possible state of the world
  • \(P(s)\) probability of state \(s\)
  • \(U(a, s)\): utility if action \(a\) is taken and state \(s\) occurs
  • \(EU(a)\): expected utility of action \(a\)

Expected utility asks: how good is this action on average, given uncertainty?

Expected utility of taking the umbrella
\[ \begin{align} EU(TakeUmbrella) &= P(Rain)U(TakeUmbrella, Rain) + P(NoRain)U(TakeUmbrella, NoRain). \\ &= 0.4 \times 70 + 0.6 \times 90 = 82 \end{align} \]

Expected utility of leaving the umbrella
\[ \begin{align} EU(LeaveUmbrella) &= P(Rain)P(LeaveUmbrella, Rain) + P(NoRain)P(LeaveUmbrella, NoRain) \\ &= 0.4 \times 0 + 0.6 \times 100 = 60 \end{align} \]

\(EU(TakeUmbrella) > EU(LeaveUmbrella)\), so the rational choice under this model is \(TakeUmbrella\).

Maximum Expected Utility(MEU)

Maximum Expected Utility
The maximum expected utility principle chooses an action with the highest expected utility:
\[ a^* = \arg \max_{a \in A} EU(a) \]

MEU does not necessarily choose

  • the most likely state;
  • the action with the best possible outcome;
  • the action that avoids all risk.

MEU chooses
The action with the best probability-weighted utility.

One-shot MEU procedure

This is the core calculation for a one-shot decision under uncertainty. Larger models may require more efficient representation and inference, but the principle is the same.

One-Off Decision Problem

One-Off Decision Problem
A one-off decision problem consists of decision variables, random variables, probabilities over random variables, and utilities over resulting outcomes. The goal is to choose the decision values that maximise expected utility.
Component Meaning
Decision variable A variable controlled by the agent
Random variable An uncertain environment factor
Probability model Describes how likely the uncertain states are
Utility function Describes the desirability of final outcomes
Solution A decision with maximum expected utility

Worked Example: Delivery Robot

A delivery robot chooses whether to wear protective pads and whether to take a short or long route. The chance of an accident depends on the route, and the final utility depends on the route, the accident outcome, and whether the robot wears pads.

One-Shot vs Sequential Decisions

One-Shot Decision Problem
A one-shot decision problem requires the agent to choose one action or one assignment of decision variables under uncertainty.
Sequential Decision Problem
A sequential decision problem requires the agent to choose actions over time, where current actions affect future states, observations, and rewards.
