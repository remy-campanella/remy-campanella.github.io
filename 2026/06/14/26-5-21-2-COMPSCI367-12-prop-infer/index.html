Case Study: A RescueBot Reasoning Problem

Scenario
A rescue robot enters a damaged research facility. The facility has rooms connected in a grid. Some hazards (fire or gas leakage) are hidden.

What the robot knows
The robot cannot see hazards through walls. It receives local sensor readings in its current room.

The robot can detect heat, or chemical smell to sense these hazards in neighbouring rooms.

Decision question
Can it safely move into room \((2, 1)\)?

From Entailment to Inference

Semantic entailment (\(KB \models \alpha\)): Every model of KB satisfies \(\alpha\).

Proof procedure (\(KB \vdash \alpha\)): Algorithm that derives \(\alpha\) from KB.

  • Input: a propositional knowledge base KB and a query \(\alpha\).

  • Output: decide whether \(KB \nvDash \alpha\)

  • Soundness: If \(KB \vdash \alpha\) then \(KB \models \alpha\).

  • Completeness: If \(KB \models \alpha\) then \(KB \vdash \alpha\).

Satisfiability & Contradiction

A formula is satisfiable if at least one model makes it true. A formula is unsatisfiable if no model makes it true.

  • Satisfiable: \(P \land Q\), model: \(P = \text{T}, Q = \text{F}\)
  • Unsatisfiable: \(P \land \neg P\)

Entailment via contradiction
\(KB \models \alpha\) iff \(KB \land \neg \alpha\) is unsatisfiable.

If adding the negated query leads to contradiction, every model of the original knowledge base satisfies the query.

Clauses and CNF

A literal is an atom or its negation. Examples: \(H_{11}, \neg H_{11}, F_{21}, \neg G_{21}\).

A clause is a disjunction of literals. Example: \(H_{11} \lor \neg F_{21}\).

A formula is in conjunctive normal form when it is a conjunction of clauses.
\[ (H_{11} \lor \neg F_{21}) \land (M_{11} \lor \neg G_{21}) \land (F_{21} \lor G_{21} \lor \mathsf{Safe}_{21}) \]

Resolution

The resolution rule combines two clauses that contain complementary literals:
\[ (A \lor P), (B \lor \neg P) \Rightarrow A \lor B \]

Resolution by Contradiction

The empty clause \(\Box\) contains no literals and represents contradiction.

Proof method
To prove
\[ KB \models \alpha \]

convert
\[ KB \cup \{\alpha\} \]

to clauses and use resolution to derive
\[ \Box \]

This proves that \(KB \land \neg \alpha\) is unsatisfiable.

Rescuebot resolution proof
Prove \(KB |= \mathsf{Safe}_{21}\) by adding \(\neg \mathsf{Safe}_{21}\) and deriving \(\Box\).

  • Negated query: \(\neg \text{Safe}_{21}\)
  • Clauses:
    \(\neg H_{11},\; H_{11} \lor \neg F_{21},\; \neg M_{11},\; M_{11} \lor \neg G_{21},\; F_{21} \lor G_{21} \lor \text{Safe}_{21},\; \neg \text{Safe}_{21}\)
  • Derive \(\neg F_{21}, \neg G_{21}, F_{21} \lor G_{21}, G_{21}, \square\) → \(\mathsf{Safe}_{21}\) is entailed.

Horn Clauses & Definite Clauses

A Horn clause is a clause with at most one positive literal.

A definite clause is a Horn clause with exactly one positive literal. It can be written as
\[ H \leftarrow A_1 \land \dots \land A_m \]

If \(m = 0\): it’s a fact.

RescueBot in Horn form (renamed):

Original Horn equivalent
\(\neg H_{11}\) NoHeat11
\(\neg M_{11}\) NoSmell11
\(H_{11} \lor \neg F_{21}\) NoFire21 ← NoHeat11
\(M_{11} \lor \neg G_{21}\) NoGas21 ← NoSmell11
\(F_{21} \lor G_{21} \lor \text{Safe}_{21}\) Safe21 ← NoFire21 NoGas21

Forward Chaining

Forward Chaining
Firward chaining is a data-driven inference procedure for definite clauses. It starts from known facts and repeatedly applies rules whose premises are already known.

For a rule
\[ H \leftarrow A_1 \and A_2 \and \ldots A_n \]

If \(A_1, \ldots, A_n\) are known, add \(H\).

Backward Chaining

Backward chaining is a goal-driven inference procedure for definite clauses. It starts with a query and tries to prove the premises of rules that could conclude the query.

Goal tree for Safe21?
Safe21 ← NoFire21 NoGas21
NoFire21 ← NoHeat11
NoGas21 ← NoSmell11
All subgoals satisfied → query succeeds.

Key Definitions Recap

Term Meaning
Literal Atom or its negation
Clause Disjunction of literals
CNF Conjunction of clauses
Resolution Inference rule for clauses
Empty clause (\(\square\)) Contradiction
Horn clause ≤ 1 positive literal
Definite clause Exactly 1 positive literal
Forward chaining Facts → consequences
Backward chaining Query → subgoals → facts
